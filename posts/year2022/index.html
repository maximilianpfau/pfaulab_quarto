Our three most important publications in 2022

1. Photoreceptor loss and influence of genetics in Stargardt disease

Pfau M […] Brooks BP. Photoreceptor degeneration in ABCA4‑associated retinopathy and its genetic correlates. JCI Insight. 2022 Jan 25;7(2):e155373 – Link

Background: Stargardt disease is the most common inherited cause of loss of visual acuity in working age. Individual, smaller studies previously showed that optical coherence tomography could roughly categorise the loss of photoreceptors.

Innovation: In the study, which is based on data from a natural history study at the National Eye Institute (project leader: Brian P. Brooks, MD, PhD), we showed that it is possible using a self‑developed artificial intelligence‑based approach to precisely assess the loss of photoreceptors over time. Furthermore, we demonstrated that the different variants in the ABCA4 gene, all of which cause Stargardt disease, influence the progression of the disease.

Significance: ==The work contributes to the use of optical coherence tomography‑based evaluations in ongoing and upcoming therapeutic studies.== We will also investigate individual genetic subtypes of Stargardt disease in future studies.

2. Dark adaptation in age‑related macular degeneration

Hess K […] Cukras CA. Evaluation of Cone‑ and Rod‑Mediated Parameters in Dark Adaptation Testing as Outcome Measures in Age‑Related Macular Degeneration. Ophthalmology Retina. 2022 May 26:S2468‑6530(22)00252‑4 – Link

Background: For age‑related macular degeneration it has been known for several years that dark adaptation — for example getting used to the dark when entering a tunnel — is severely restricted. There are several clinical metrics to assess dark adaptation, but they have not been adequately compared to date.

Innovation: This investigation — based on a natural history study at the National Eye Institute (project leader: Catherine A. Cukras, MD, PhD) — demonstrated that the “rod‑intercept time” (time until retinal sensitivity reaches a certain threshold again) can be measured most reliably and shows the closest relationship to disease progression. The study also showed in a large longitudinal cohort that restricted function of cone photoreceptors (cells for daytime vision) can only be measured later in the course.

Significance: ==This publication makes a significant contribution to understanding the temporal sequence of functional loss in age‑related macular degeneration.== The now much better understanding of the precision of the metrics helps us to assess disease progression more accurately in the future and to plan therapy studies.

3. Therapeutic effect of pegcetacoplan in age‑related macular degeneration

Pfau M […] Holz FG. Association of complement C3 inhibitor pegcetacoplan with reduced photoreceptor degeneration beyond areas of geographic atrophy. Scientific Reports. 2022 Oct 25;12(1):17870 – Link

Background: Age‑related macular degeneration is the most common cause of central vision loss. There is still no approved therapy. While it was already apparent that the drug slows the spread of areas with severe tissue loss (“geographic atrophy”), the effect on the remaining retina was less clear.

Innovation: The artificial intelligence‑based analysis, conducted with data from the international Phase 2 study (https://clinicaltrials.gov/ct2/show/NCT02503332), showed that not only areas of geographic atrophy progress more slowly. The photoreceptor layers in the surrounding retina also thin less over time.

Significance: ==The study suggests that pegcetacoplan not only slows the progression of geographic atrophy but also protects the more distant retina.== In future scientific studies, it should therefore be investigated whether earlier stages of age‑related macular degeneration are also treatable.
